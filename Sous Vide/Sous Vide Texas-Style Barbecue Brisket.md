# Sous Vide Texas-Style Barbecue Brisket

Cooking sous vide turns tough, lean brisket into moist, tender meat that normally takes expert-level smoking skill to pull off. A shot of liquid smoke in the bag adds smokiness, and finishing in the oven or on a grill builds the thick, crisp bark that mirrors traditional barbecue. The result is consistent, repeatable, fall-apart brisket — no overnight fire-tending required.

### 🛒 Ingredients
- 2 ounces coarsely ground black peppercorns (about ⅓ cup; 55 g); see tip
- 2 ¼ ounces kosher salt (about ¼ cup; 65 g)
- ¼ ounce (10 g) pink salt, such as Prague Powder Curing Salt (optional — for a pink smoke ring)
- 1 flat-cut or point-cut brisket, about 5 pounds (2.25 kg), fat cap intact and well marbled
- ¼ teaspoon liquid smoke, such as Wright's Liquid Smoke (optional)
- Dill pickles, sliced yellow onion, and white bread, for serving

### 🥣 Steps
1. Combine pepper, salt, and pink salt (if using) in a small bowl. Rub two-thirds of the mixture evenly over the surface of the brisket; reserve the remaining one-third. Slice the brisket in half crosswise so it fits into large vacuum bags.
2. Place each brisket half in a vacuum bag. (Fold the top of each bag over while you add the brisket so no rub or juices get on the sealing edge.) Add 4 drops (about ⅛ teaspoon) liquid smoke, if using, to each bag. Seal with a vacuum sealer and rest in the refrigerator for 2 to 3 hours.
3. Set your precision cooker to **135°F (57°C)** for a tender, steak-like texture, or **155°F (68°C)** for a more traditional texture that falls apart when pulled. Add the brisket to the water bath and cover (lid, foil, or ping pong balls). Cook 24 to 36 hours at 155°F, or 36 to 72 hours at 135°F. Let the cooked brisket cool at least to room temperature before finishing (an ice bath speeds this up). It can be refrigerated at this stage for up to 1 week.
4. **To finish on the grill:** Light ½ chimney of charcoal; when covered with gray ash, bank the coals to one side. Set the grate in place, cover, and preheat 5 minutes (or set half the burners of a gas grill to medium-high and preheat 10 minutes). Clean and oil the grate.
5. Remove the brisket from the bags and blot dry with paper towels. (The bag liquid can be simmered into your favorite barbecue sauce for extra flavor.) Rub the reserved salt-and-pepper mixture into the surface. Place the brisket on the cooler side of the grill, fat cap up. Add 4 to 5 hardwood chunks to the hotter side (wrap loosely in foil if using gas). Cover and smoke, adjusting vents to hold 275–300°F (135–149°C) and adding 2 to 3 more wood chunks twice during cooking, until a deep, dark bark forms, about 3 hours. Continue with step 7.
6. **To finish in the oven:** Adjust an oven rack to the lower-middle position and preheat to 300°F (150°C) (or 275°F/135°C on convection). Remove the brisket from the bags and blot dry. Rub the reserved salt-and-pepper mixture into the surface. Place fat cap up on a wire rack set in a rimmed baking sheet and roast until a deep, dark bark forms, about 2 hours. Continue with step 7.
7. Transfer the brisket to a cutting board and tent with foil. Rest until the temperature drops to 145–165°F (63–74°C), about 30 minutes. Slice against the grain into thin strips and serve with white bread, dill pickles, and sliced onion.

> [!tip] Tip
> For an even, coarse pepper grind, a manual burr coffee grinder beats a blade grinder. Use a brisket with the fat cap on and plenty of intramuscular marbling — a fully trimmed flat comes out relatively dry. Temperature/time guide: **135°F for 36–72 hr** = firm and meaty, like a tender steak; **155°F for 24–36 hr** = extra moist, with a traditional pull-apart texture.
