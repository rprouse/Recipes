# 🌍 Global Flavor Profiles

> Part of [[Cooking Reference]]

A cheat sheet of the signature spices, herbs, and pantry staples that define major food cultures — a starting point for building culturally-inspired dishes. Most cuisines are built on an **aromatic base** (the foundation you start cooking from) balanced by **accents** of sour, heat, sweet, and umami. Get the base and the balance right and a dish reads as authentic even when you're improvising.

> [!warning] These are broad strokes
> Every entry below generalizes across huge, regionally diverse cuisines (there is no single "Indian" or "Chinese" cooking). Treat these as flavor *directions*, not rules — and lean on real recipes for specific dishes.

## 🧭 How to use this for improvising

1. **Start with the flavor base.** Sweat the listed aromatics first — this alone steers a dish toward a cuisine (a garlic-ginger-scallion base reads Chinese; onion-carrot-celery reads French/Italian).
2. **Layer 2–4 signature spices**, not the whole list. A few done well beats a muddle of everything.
3. **Balance with the accents.** Add the sour / heat / sweet element listed — this is what makes many cuisines "pop" (Thai and Vietnamese especially live or die by the balance).
4. **Finish with the fresh herbs** off the heat.
5. **Anchor with a pantry staple** (the right fat, starch, or condiment) to ground it — ghee vs olive oil vs palm oil changes everything.

> [!tip] Fastest way to shift a dish's identity
> Keep the protein and technique the same and swap the **base + accent** together: the same seared chicken becomes Mexican with onion/chile + lime, Thai with lemongrass/chili + fish sauce & lime, or Italian with garlic + peperoncino & lemon.
