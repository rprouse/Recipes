# Herbs — Fresh vs Dried

> Part of [[Cooking Reference]]

Herbs split roughly into two camps that behave very differently. **Hardy/woody herbs** (rosemary, thyme, oregano) hold up to heat and time and often work well dried — add them **early**. **Tender/soft herbs** (basil, cilantro, parsley) are delicate, lose their character when cooked or dried, and are best **fresh and added at the end**.

| Herb         | Type       | Fresh or Dried?            | Best Uses                                                |
| ------------ | ---------- | -------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Rosemary** | Hardy      | Both work; dried is fine   | Roasts, potatoes, breads, braises — add early            |
| **Thyme**    | Hardy      | Both work well             | Soups, stews, roasts, sauces — add early                 |
| **Oregano**  | Hardy      | Dried is often *preferred* | Tomato sauces, pizza, Greek & Mexican dishes             |
| **Sage**     | Hardy      | Both work                  | Poultry, stuffing, brown-butter sauces, sausage          |
| **Bay leaf** | Hardy      | Almost always dried        | Soups, stews, stocks, braises — remove before serving    |
| **Marjoram** | Semi-hardy | Both                       | Similar to oregano but milder; sauces, meats             |
| **Basil**    | Tender     | **Fresh** (dried is weak)  | Pesto, tomatoes, pasta, finishing — add at the end       |
| **Cilantro** | Tender     | **Fresh only**             | Salsas, Mexican & Asian dishes, garnish — add at the end |
| **Parsley**  | Tender     | **Fresh**                  | Garnish, tabbouleh, sauces, brightening finished dishes  |
| **Dill**     | Tender     | Fresh (dried okay)         | Fish, potatoes, pickles, yogurt sauces                   |
| **Mint**     | Tender     | **Fresh**                  | Salads, drinks, Middle Eastern & Thai dishes, desserts   |
| **Chives**   | Tender     | **Fresh**                  | Eggs, potatoes, soups, garnish                           |
| **Tarragon** | Tender     | Fresh (dried okay)         | Chicken, fish, eggs, French sauces (béarnaise)           |

### 🌿 Key tips
- **Conversion:** dried herbs are more concentrated — use about **1 tsp dried = 1 tbsp fresh** (roughly a **1:3** ratio).
- **Timing:** add **dried and hardy herbs early** so they soften and release flavour; add **fresh, tender herbs at the end** so they stay bright.
- **Dried tender herbs are usually a waste** — dried basil, parsley, and cilantro lose most of their aroma. Buy those fresh.
- **Bloom dried herbs** in oil or butter over heat to wake up their flavour.
- **Woody stems** (rosemary, thyme) can be dropped in whole while cooking and pulled out — or stripped, since the leaves hold the flavour.

### 🗄️ Storage
- **Tender herbs:** trim stems and stand in a glass of water like flowers, loosely covered, in the fridge (basil prefers the counter). Or wrap in a damp paper towel.
- **Hardy herbs:** wrap in a slightly damp paper towel in the fridge; they last 1–2 weeks.
- **Dried herbs:** keep in a cool, dark cupboard away from the stove's heat; replace after **~1 year** — they fade, they don't spoil.
- **Freeze extras:** chop and freeze tender herbs in oil or water in ice-cube trays.

> [!tip] Rule of thumb
> **Hardy herbs → early and can be dried. Tender herbs → fresh and at the end.** When substituting dried for fresh, use about **one-third** as much.
