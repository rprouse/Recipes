# ♨️ Sous-Vide Times & Temperatures

> Part of [[Cooking Reference]]

The one idea that unlocks sous-vide: **temperature sets doneness, time sets texture.** Because the water bath never exceeds your target temp, food can't overcook past that doneness — so you dial in *how done* with the temperature, and *how tender* (and how thick) with the time. That splits meats into two camps: **tender cuts** (steaks, chops, fish) you cook just long enough to heat through, and **tough cuts** (chuck, brisket, ribs) you hold for many hours so collagen melts to gelatin without drying out.

> [!warning] Food safety — read before the low temps tempt you
> Sous-vide makes food safe by **time *and* temperature together (pasteurization)**, not temperature alone. Below **54.4 °C / 130 °F**, bacteria can grow — don't hold food in that zone beyond ~2–3 hours total. At 55 °C/131 °F and up, longer cooks pasteurize as they go (thicker/lower = more time needed). Poultry cooked below 74 °C/165 °F *is* safe **if held long enough** at temp — the charts below assume that. When unsure, cook hotter or shorter, and follow a trusted pasteurization table (Douglas Baldwin's is the standard). Temps and times here are **approximate**, rounded from common references, and vary with your gear and the exact cut.

## 🥩 Beef & Lamb — tender cuts (steaks, tenderloin, rack)

Pick the **temperature** for doneness; pick the **time** from the thickness table below.

| Doneness | Temperature | Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rare | 49–52 °C / 120–125 °F | Very soft, translucent |
| **Medium-rare** *(classic)* | 54 °C / 129 °F | Tender, juicy, warm red centre |
| Medium | 57–60 °C / 135–140 °F | Firmer, pink |
| Medium-well | 60–63 °C / 140–145 °F | Mostly grey-brown, still moist |
| Well done | 65–68 °C / 149–155 °F | Firm, cooked through |

## ⏱️ Time by thickness — tender cuts (heating through)

Measured from fridge temperature. The **minimum** heats the centre to target; you can hold longer for extra tenderness, but tender steaks turn mushy past ~4 hours.

| Thickness | From fridge (min) | From frozen (+~50–60%) | Max hold before texture suffers |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ½ in / 13 mm | ~45 min | ~1 hr 10 min | ~2–2½ hr |
| 1 in / 25 mm | ~1 hr | ~1½ hr | ~3 hr |
| 1½ in / 40 mm | ~2 hr | ~3 hr | ~4 hr |
| 2 in / 50 mm | ~3 hr | ~4½ hr | ~4–5 hr |
| 2½ in / 65 mm | ~4 hr | ~6 hr | ~5–6 hr |
| 3 in / 75 mm | ~5 hr | ~7½ hr | ~6 hr |

## 🍖 Beef & Lamb — tough cuts (long, collagen-melting cooks)

These need hours at temp to become tender; a frozen start is negligible over such long times.

| Cut | Temperature | Time |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chuck / blade "steak" (steak-like, sliceable) | 55–57 °C / 131–135 °F | 24–36 hr |
| Chuck / pot roast (fall-apart) | 74 °C / 165 °F | 18–24 hr |
| Brisket (sliceable, med-rare) | 57–60 °C / 135–140 °F | 36–48 hr |
| Brisket (traditional, tender) | 68 °C / 155 °F | 24–36 hr |
| Beef short ribs (steak-like) | 57–60 °C / 135–140 °F | 48–72 hr |
| Beef short ribs (fall-off-bone) | 74 °C / 165 °F | 12–24 hr |
| Lamb shank / shoulder | 63–68 °C / 145–155 °F | 24–48 hr |

## 🐟 Fish & Seafood

Delicate and quick — most fish is soft and buttery at low temps, flaky toward the top. Overholding turns fish pasty, so stay near the minimum.

| Item | Temperature | Time |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Salmon (buttery / medium) | 43–50 °C / 110–122 °F | 30–45 min |
| White fish — cod, halibut, bass | 50–55 °C / 122–131 °F | 30–45 min |
| Tuna (rare, seared after) | 43–46 °C / 110–115 °F | 30–45 min |
| Shrimp | 55–60 °C / 131–140 °F | 20–35 min |
| Scallops | 50 °C / 122 °F | 30–45 min |
| Lobster / crab | 55 °C / 131 °F | 45–60 min |
| Octopus (tender) | 77 °C / 171 °F | 4–5 hr |

## 🥚 Eggs

Eggs are the sous-vide showpiece — small temperature shifts change everything. Cooked **in the shell**.

| Style | Temperature | Time |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Runny, barely set (for ramen/toast) | 60 °C / 140 °F | 60 min |
| Classic "63° egg" — jammy yolk, silky white | 63 °C / 145 °F | 45–60 min |
| Firmer custard yolk, set white | 65 °C / 149 °F | 45–60 min |
| Hard-cooked (peels clean) | 75 °C / 167 °F | 12–15 min |

## 🥕 Vegetables

Vegetables are the exception to the low-temp rule: their cell walls (pectin) only break down around **83–85 °C / 183–185 °F**, so they cook *hotter* than most meats.

| Type | Temperature | Time |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Root veg — carrots, beets, turnips | 84 °C / 183 °F | 1–2 hr |
| Potatoes | 85 °C / 185 °F | 1–2 hr |
| Winter squash | 85 °C / 185 °F | 1–2 hr |
| Green veg — asparagus, green beans, broccoli | 84 °C / 183 °F | 10–20 min |
| Corn on the cob | 84 °C / 183 °F | 30 min |

## ♨️ Key tips
- **Temperature = doneness, time = tenderness.** Want it more done? Raise the temp. Want a tough cut softer? Give it more time — not more heat.
- **From frozen: add roughly 50–60% to the time** (see the thickness table). No need to thaw tender cuts first; for long tough-cut cooks the frozen start barely matters. Thin fish only needs ~10–15 min extra.
- **Sear *after*, not before.** Sous-vide gives no browning. Dry the surface thoroughly, then sear hard and fast — screaming-hot pan, torch, or grill for 30–60 sec a side — so you get crust without cooking the interior further.
- **Don't hold tender cuts too long.** Past the "max hold" column, muscle fibres over-tenderize and the texture turns mushy. Tough cuts are the opposite — they *need* the long hold.
- **Salt timing:** seasoning in the bag is fine for cooks under a few hours; for very long cooks (8 hr+), salt can cure the surface to a ham-like texture — season lightly, or salt after.
- **Bag smart:** vacuum sealer is ideal; otherwise use the water-displacement method with a zip-top freezer bag. Add aromatics (herbs, garlic, a little fat) to the bag, but go easy on raw garlic over long cooks — it can turn harsh.
- **Chilling & holding:** to cook ahead, ice-bath the sealed bag straight from the water and refrigerate; reheat in the bath at serving temp before searing.

> [!tip] Rule of thumb
> **Set the temperature for how *done* you want it, and the time for how *thick* and how *tender*.** Tender cuts: an hour or two, sear after. Tough cuts: 24–72 hours. Vegetables: 84 °C/183 °F. Cooking from frozen? Add about half again the time.
