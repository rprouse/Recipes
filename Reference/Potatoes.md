# Potatoes — Types & Best Uses

> Part of [[Cooking Reference]]

Potatoes are grouped mostly by **starch content**, which decides how they cook. **Starchy** potatoes are fluffy and absorbent (great for baking and frying), **waxy** potatoes are firm and hold their shape (great for boiling and salads), and **all-purpose** potatoes sit in the middle.

| Potato Type | Common Names / Varieties | Best Uses |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Starchy** (high starch, low moisture) | Russet, Idaho, Maris Piper, King Edward | French fries, baked/jacket potatoes, fluffy mashed potatoes, roasting, potato wedges, gnocchi |
| **All-purpose / Medium-starch** | Yukon Gold, other yellow potatoes, white potatoes | Creamy mashed potatoes, roasting, gratins/scalloped, pan-frying, soups — the do-everything potato |
| **Waxy** (low starch, high moisture) | Red potatoes (Red Bliss), new potatoes, Charlotte | Potato salad, boiling, soups & stews, roasting, casseroles — holds its shape and won't turn mushy |
| **Fingerling** (waxy) | Russian Banana, French Fingerling, La Ratte | Roasting, potato salad, pan-roasting whole or halved, serving as a firm side |
| **Petite / Baby** (waxy) | Baby reds, baby Yukons, creamer potatoes | Boiling, roasting whole, salads, quick weeknight sides |
| **Purple / Blue** (mostly waxy) | Purple Peruvian, All Blue, Adirondack Blue | Roasting, salads, colourful sides, mash for visual contrast — keeps colour best roasted |
| **Sweet potato** (not a true potato) | Garnet, Jewel, Beauregard, Japanese (Murasaki) | Baking, roasting, fries, mash, pies and other baking |

### 🗄️ Storage
- **Keep cool, dark, and dry.** A pantry, cellar, or cupboard around **7–10 °C (45–50 °F)** is ideal. Good airflow matters — store them in a paper bag, burlap sack, or basket, never a sealed plastic bag (trapped moisture rots them).
- **Don't refrigerate raw potatoes.** Cold turns their starch to sugar, giving a gritty texture and causing them to brown and taste sweet when cooked (and the sugars can form more acrylamide when fried).
- **Keep them out of the light.** Light triggers greening and sprouting. Green skin means **solanine**, a bitter, mildly toxic compound — cut away green parts and sprouts generously, or discard if there's a lot.
- **Store away from onions.** Both give off gases and moisture that make the other spoil faster.
- **Don't wash until you're ready to cook.** Moisture speeds up rot; brush off dirt and wash just before use.
- **Shelf life:** mature potatoes (russets, Yukons) keep for **weeks to a couple of months** stored well; thin-skinned new/baby potatoes are more perishable — use within **1–2 weeks**.
- **Sweet potatoes** prefer slightly warmer storage (~13–15 °C / 55–60 °F) and, like regular potatoes, should not be refrigerated.
- **Cooked potatoes** keep in the fridge **3–4 days** in a sealed container. Mashed and cooked potatoes freeze reasonably well; raw ones don't.

> [!tip] Quick rule of thumb
> If you want it **fluffy** (mash, bake, fry) reach for a **russet**. If you want it to **hold together** (salad, boil, stew) reach for a **waxy red or new potato**. When in doubt, **Yukon Gold** does almost everything well.
